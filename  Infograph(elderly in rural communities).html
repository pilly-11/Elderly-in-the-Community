Under the Australian Charter of Healthcare Rights, every person — regardless of where they live — has the right to access, safety, respect, partnership, information, privacy, and feedback. Rural older Australians face systemic barriers that threaten these rights.

Australian Charter of Healthcare Rights
Rights for Rural Older Australians

Right to Access

Every older Australian has the right to healthcare regardless of location. Rural older people are entitled to equitable access to GPs, specialists, and emergency care — yet geographic and transport barriers persistently obstruct this fundamental right.2,4

(AIHW, 2023; ACSQHC, 2022)

Right to Safety

Older Australians have the right to receive safe care. Extended ambulance response times and greater distances to hospitals can compromise this right — particularly during cardiac or stroke emergencies.5

(Stirling et al., 2020)

Right to Partnership

Rural older people have the right to be active partners in their healthcare decisions. Health professionals play a key role supporting health literacy, medication management, and empowering informed decision-making.4

(ACSQHC, 2022)

Right to Information

Older Australians have the right to clear, timely health information. Social isolation and digital exclusion in rural areas limit access, making community education and telehealth critical tools.6

(Iecovich, 2014)

Limited Healthcare Access

(AIHW, 2023)

Transport Barriers

Limited public transport; reliance on others to reach care.3

(COTA, 2022)

Social Isolation

Distance from family and smaller populations affects mental wellbeing.6

(Iecovich, 2014)

Chronic Disease Management

Ongoing conditions need regular monitoring — harder to access rurally.2

(AIHW, 2023)

Emergency Response Times

Longer ambulance times and greater distances worsen outcomes.5

(Stirling et al., 2020)
